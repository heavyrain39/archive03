<p>Master, as tokenized assets (RWA) grow, there is a name that appears frequently. That is <strong>Chainlink ($LINK)</strong>.</p>

<p>Chainlink is not a flashy consumer app. In fact, it's actually quite boring. It is a project that handles the <strong>plumbing</strong>—connecting price data, external information, cross-chain messages, and financial institutions with the blockchain.</p>

<p>However, in the world of financial infrastructure, it is often the boring plumbing that collects fees for the longest time.</p>

{{< char name="mikael" title="Mikael" >}}
<p>Kurumi, Chainlink's role is certainly important. However, Human, you must be careful not to treat "important technology" and an "appreciating token" as the same thing.</p>

<p>First, there is the issue of value capture. Even if Chainlink's technology is widely adopted, we must separately examine how directly that revenue and value return to LINK holders. Network usage and token price do not always move in tandem.</p>

<p>Second, institutions prefer their own infrastructure. Large banks and depositories may want to maintain direct control over core functions even if they use external protocols. Even if Chainlink becomes a standard, revenue sharing could be limited.</p>

<p>Third, competition. Oracles, interoperability, messaging, custody, and payment infrastructure are all areas that smell of profit. LayerZero, Wormhole, and private chain solutions could all become formidable competitors.</p>

<p>Fourth, RWA itself is still in its early stages. While tokenized treasuries and funds are growing, legal ownership, liquidity, and regulatory issues are not yet fully resolved. If the RWA market moves slowly, Chainlink's re-evaluation may also be slow.</p>

<p>My risk score is <strong>70/100</strong>. Chainlink is a strong infrastructure candidate, but investors must look more critically at the token economics and actual monetization strategies.</p>
