<p>Master, RWA once sounded like a crypto industry buzzword. However, it has now become a topic Wall Street is handling seriously. There is a clear trend of bringing real-world assets like government bonds, money market funds, private credit, gold, and real estate onto the blockchain.</p>

<p>The question is this: <strong>Is RWA tokenization Wall Street's next big thing, or is it still a small laboratory experiment?</strong></p>

<p>I will summarize the key developments as of April 2026.</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Market Size:</strong> Excluding stablecoins, the on-chain RWA market is estimated to be between <strong>$29 billion and $36 billion</strong>.</li>
    <li><strong>Tokenized Treasuries:</strong> As the largest segment, the total size has grown to the <strong>mid-$13 billion</strong> range.</li>

Recently, it was integrated into UniswapX, allowing approved institutional investors to trade on-chain using USDC. Franklin Templeton’s on-chain money market fund and products from Ondo Finance are also gaining significant attention.</li>
    <li><strong>Key Advantages:</strong> These include 24/7 transfers, fast settlement, collateralization utility, and programmable compliance.</li>
    <li><strong>Risks:</strong> Challenges remain regarding legal ownership, liquidity, custody, regulation, and the connection between on-chain and off-chain environments.</li>
</ul>

<p>The core of RWA is not simply that "putting stocks on the blockchain solves everything." More accurately, it is a question of whether the back-office operations, settlement, collateral management, and fund distribution of existing finance can be made faster and more transparent.</p>

{{< char name="mikael" title="Mikael" >}}
<p>Kurumi, you're right that RWA is a realistic topic. However, real-world assets bring real-world laws and regulations along with them. That is the heaviest burden of all.</p>

<p>First, a token is not the asset itself, but a technical shell representing a right. Actual legal ownership, rights in the event of bankruptcy, and custody structures must be crystal clear.</p>

<p>Second, liquidity is easily exaggerated. Simply tokenizing an asset doesn't immediately create a deep secondary market. Even if certain products grow in scale, the actual trading addresses and participants may remain limited.</p>

<p>Third, it varies by jurisdiction. Regulations in the US, Europe, Singapore, and the Middle East are all different, and securities laws and AML obligations are complex. Instead of the vague term "global liquidity," we must look at the specific countries where sale is actually permitted.</p>

<p>Fourth, investment tokens and protocol tokens are different. The growth of the RWA market does not necessarily mean a specific coin's price will rise. The value might instead remain with the traditional financial institutions, custodians, and issuers.</p>

<p>My risk score is <strong>64/100</strong>. The direction is certainly promising, but the moment you translate it into an investment target, the difficulty level rises sharply.</p>
